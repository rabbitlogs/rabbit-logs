Hi, this is Rabbit! 🐰

After my [SAP certification post](/en/blog/sap-certification), many readers asked what else they should study to grow as an SAP expert. Today's answer is a certification closely tied to SAP: **CPIM** (Certified in Planning and Inventory Management).

Among working SAP PP (production planning) professionals, it's often recommended even more strongly than an SAP certification. I say this from personal experience.

> **In 3 lines**
> - CPIM covers the principles of supply chain management — the process, not a specific system.
> - If an SAP certification is about "How" (implementing a system), CPIM is about "Why" (the reasoning behind it).
> - It demands real time and money, but it widens a practitioner's view from system to business.

[[TOC]]

## "You have the order backwards"

This goes back to my first SAP PP build project, when I was still struggling to keep up. As the project neared its end, I decided it was time to earn an SAP PP certification.

So I asked the project's lead — a PP consultant with 25 years of experience — for advice. "I want to grow as an SAP expert. Should I start with the PP certification?" I expected a yes. The answer surprised me.

He was blunt. "You have the order backwards. SAP is just a tool that captured the ERP market. Memorizing T-codes and configuration values is quick to learn. But if you don't understand **why** S&OP flows into the master production schedule, **why** the MRP logic unfolds the way it does, you're not an expert — just an operator. Study CPIM before the SAP certification."

Today I'll unpack why he said that.

## CPIM vs SAP certification: 'Why' and 'How'

The biggest difference is focus.

- **An SAP certification** centers on application functions — **how** to configure and implement one specific system, SAP.
- **CPIM** centers on business process — **why** a process should exist at all, covering the global standards and principles of supply chain management.

The key point is that CPIM is **vendor-neutral**. Its knowledge applies to any ERP — SAP, Oracle, Dynamics. An SAP certification, by contrast, is system knowledge valid only inside the SAP ecosystem.

In restaurant terms, an SAP certification is "how to operate this kitchen's oven," while CPIM is "the principles of cooking." Oven controls have to be relearned in a new kitchen; the principles of cooking carry everywhere.

## Why 'principles' build trust

The real reason that consultant emphasized CPIM comes down to trust. An ERP consultant is essentially a translator, turning a client's business needs (reality) into system logic (SAP). CPIM knowledge decides the quality of that translation.

Say a production manager says, "Because of a bottleneck at machine A, we have to adjust the weekly schedule by hand."

A **How-only consultant** replies, "SAP's standard assumes infinite capacity, so that's hard. Please follow the standard." The client walks away thinking "IT doesn't understand the floor."

A **Why-aware consultant** answers differently: "So machine A is a capacity-constrained resource. Then we should apply finite capacity scheduling — using PP/DS, or adjusting MRP logic and lot size within the standard to get a similar effect." Only then does the client feel truly understood.

Trust doesn't come from looking knowledgeable. It comes from redefining a client's messy problem in standard terms and showing genuine understanding. CPIM is that shared language.

## What CPIM teaches

CPIM is an international certification in supply chain and inventory management, administered by ASCM. A single comprehensive exam covers eight modules — and they line up remarkably well with SAP's core modules.

Supply chain strategy, S&OP, demand, supply, detailed scheduling, inventory, distribution, and quality/continuous improvement — these eight are essentially the logical flow of enterprise SCM operations. Each maps to the reasoning behind SAP's PP, MM, SD, and QM modules. Learning CPIM means understanding **why** those modules are designed the way they are.

## Cost and renewal: a serious investment

The real barrier is time and money. As of 2026:

The exam is a single comprehensive test — 150 questions over about 3.5 hours, with a passing score of 300. Study typically takes three to six months of focused effort. The bundle (learning system, exam, and a second-chance attempt) runs **about $2,230** for ASCM members, higher for non-members. Through a local partner in Korea, expect roughly 3.2–3.5 million won.

> ⚠️ **Note**: CPIM is being updated in 2026. The new bundle is on sale from February 2026, and the revised exam is available from June 1 (the current exam ends May 31). If you plan to sit for it, check which version applies.

And it doesn't end at passing. You must renew every **five years by earning 75 professional development points (CEUs)**; miss the window and the certification expires, forcing a retake. Much like the SAP certification's annual renewal, CPIM is a credential you maintain.

## Rabbit's Takeaway

CPIM isn't an alternative to an SAP certification. It's closer to a multiplier on an SAP expert's career. Where SAP covers "how to implement," CPIM teaches "why it should be done that way."

It points exactly where this blog keeps pointing: process and the flow of work, over code. If you want to use SAP as a practitioner who understands the business — not just an operator — CPIM is worth serious thought.
